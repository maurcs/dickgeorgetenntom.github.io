Spirits, music, drama - and Nixon? (10/25)


Thursday, October 25, 2007 10:34 AM CDT

Along with a chill wind and October's end comes the second Ghosts & Legends Tour Oct. 26-27. These 90 minute excursions presented by the Columbus Convention and Visitors Bureau and Columbus Arts Council are spine-tingling entertainment for the whole family.

Tickets for the bus tours to meet some of the city's long-gone legends and spirits are $10 and “going faster than we can write the names down” according to CAC executive director Heather Rowland.

If interested in any of the four tours leaving from the Tennessee Williams Welcome Center Friday and Saturday evening, stop reading now and call (662) 328-2787 because, as this column goes to print, a very limited number of seats are left. Tickets, if available, may also be purchased online at www.columbus-arts.com.

Waiting to greet you along the journey through Columbus' past will be notable characters such as Matilda Morton of Twelve Gables, one of the famous “Decoration Day Ladies.” Don't let the fact that “Miss Mat” lived here over 140 years ago bother you.

“Any time you've got a historic place with so many characters as Columbus had, you'll have stories,” said local historian Carolyn Burns. Burns, who works in the architectural firm of historical renovation specialist Sam Kaye, researched the tales handed down through generations and planned the tour route. She and Chris Hannon will act as able guides.

“The tour has been enhanced,” Burns explained. “We've added more stories and stops, so even if you came last year, there is plenty to enjoy.” Stories aren't the only addition. Official Ghosts & Legends T-shirts are available at the Welcome Center for $15 this year.

The tours sold out in their debut last year, and look well on their way to doing it again. Proving, as we surely suspected all along, that when it comes to seeking out the mysterious and fascinating, we often need look no further than the aging pages of our own local history.

Volunteers for Christ concert Oct. 27

Music, comedy, and a powerful message will dominate the ninth annual Volunteers for Christ rally at Trotter Convention Center in Columbus Saturday evening beginning at 6 p.m.

According to co-organizer Rhonda Richardson, thanks to corporate sponsors Johnston Tombigbee, North Atlantic Security and other generous supporters, admission is free. “And we're giving away a 27-inch flat screen television donated by Wal-Mart at the end of the concert. You have to be present to win,” Richardson stressed. “We've also got approximately 35 great door prizes.”

The Tyler Marsh Band is slated to kick off at 6:15 p.m., followed by Rudy Ward (aka “U-neek Message”), Redemption, the comedy skit “Oh No Lord & Family” and Plea 33. Dallas Pitts and James Godfrey will speak.

Concessions will be available. All proceeds from the event will benefit “The Brothers,” a local nonprofit organization working with youth, providing activities ranging from ACT and basic money-management workshops to organized sports opportunities these young people wouldn't otherwise have.

For more about the interdenominational Volunteers for Christ, or about the Oct. 27 event, call (662) 574-8655 or 328-6567.

Tonight

Mother Nature was not kind to Afternoon Tunes last Thursday, tripping weather sirens and canceling the Brice Miller Thank You Columbus party planned at Trotter Courtyard. She will hopefully be more benevolent today for the final Tunes of 2007 as Mike Cooper and Sheila Heatherly go on stage from 5-7 p.m.

Also tonight, as outlined last Thursday, three different theater performances offer laughter, tears and suspense.

To recap, the Columbus Arts Council presents the comedic “All the Way From Magnolia Springs” at Rent Auditorium at 7 p.m. Tickets are $20 at the door, or $10 for students or seniors, by request.

In Cromwell Theater on the Mississippi University for Women campus tonight, the Department of Theatre presents “Wit,” the Pulitzer-winning play by Margaret Edson that revolves around the central character's battle with cancer. Admission is $10 for 7:30 p.m. performances Oct. 25-27 and at 2 p.m. Oct. 28, $5 for students, or $7 for advance tickets purchased at Shattuck Hall.

For those in the mood for a dose of suspenseful romance, or romantic suspense as the case may be, Starkville Community Theatre's run of “Laura” opens tonight at the Playhouse on Main in Starkville. For ticket information, contact the box office at (662) 323-6855. Performances run Oct. 25-28 and Oct. 30-Nov. 2.

Tenn-Tom film

The Short Circuit Traveling Film Festival Oct. 29 at the Bama Theatre in Tuscaloosa, Ala., will screen 12 short films by filmmakers living and working in the Southeast. Of particular interest among them is a mini-documentary called “Dick-George, Tenn-Tom.”

The film's promo reads, “In 1971, President Richard M. Nixon visited Mobile, Ala., for 104 minutes, during which time he shook 100 feet of hands, lost a cufflink, and shared a stage with his biggest political rival, Governor George Wallace. ‘Dick-George, Tenn-Tom' is a sardonic look at their rivalry, the creation of the Tennessee-Tombigbee Waterway, and the attempt on Wallace's life less than a year later.”

Co-directors Gideon Kennedy and Marcus Rosentrater will be on hand for a question-and-answer session. Admission is $7, $6 for students and seniors. For more information, call (205) 758-4994.

In closing, “Here, Here” to the County Council of Anne Arundel County, Md., which unanimously passed a resolution for county public schools to be able to have Halloween parties - not “Fall Festival,” “Harvest Day,” “Book Character Day,” or - lamest of all - “Orange and Black Day.”

Councilman Ed Middlebrooks, apparently fed up to here with political correctness run amuck, is quoted in a Washington Post online story by William Wan, “Let's call it what it is and let the kids be kids. We have to start standing up for things like this. I don't want my kids missing out on Halloween, and I don't want them being told it's something else it's not.”

Anne Arundel County, a big bag of bite-sized Milky Ways and homemade popcorn balls to you.

And to each of you reading, Happy Halloween. May the kid in you enjoy more treats than tricks and may the adult in you remember how to tell the difference.
