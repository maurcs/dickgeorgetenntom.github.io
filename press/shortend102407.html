Conversations
Written by Noralil Ryan Fores   
Wednesday, 24 October 2007

Outside little exception, film is an art form that begs collaboration. ItÕs a creative process of back-and-forth, a shifting of images and sounds, and itÕs not one easily completed in a vacuum. And, so when Gideon Kennedy found himself with a script for the short Exclusive, he knew that he had to find both an actor and an editor. The actor, thankfully, wasnÕt hard to find. The editor, however, was a different story altogether.

ÒI had written a script and shot half of a film, a short that the script would have put at 45 minutes. Is that right?,Ó Kennedy turns to ask his film production partner. ÒFifty minutes, a hour.

ÒI had an idea that along with the script and how it would work, I thought, ÒI canÕt afford big budget stuff or an expensive look,Ó but I figured that if on the strength of my script, I could find one decent lead to do the monologue and the editing with stills interspersed with the guy giving the monologue, you could create an interesting film for very, very cheap. So, I always knew that editing was one of the key elements.

ÒI found (an actor). IÕd rehearsed him for over a month doing a 50-minute monologue straight, and he got it. We shot all that, so we had that material of him doing it three of four times. Then I sat on itÉÓ

In 2004, Marcus Rosentrater would move to Atlanta and taking a job at a video store would meet Kennedy. ÒWhen he came to the video store, one of the things I was really impressed by was not that he said he was an editor but that we would watch movies in the store, and he would watch movies that were all done by the same editor. He was clearly studying it and had that much interest in it, and so based on that, I approached him with it.

ÒI thought it was going to be a lot easier to complete than it was, and so by the end of it, it wasnÕt fair to say that I made it, and he just edited it. We really co-directed and co-produced the film,Ó Kennedy says.

ÒIt took aboutÑtoo long,Ó Rosentrater adds, the two laughing. ÒBut, (Gideon) moved to Mobile shortly after we started, and so our partnership since then has been long distance. We produce everything apart from one another.Ó

With limited technical resources but with a great precision in research and crafting, Kennedy and Rosentrater here talk about their satrical documentary Dick-George, Tenn-Tom, Porky Pig-esque speech patterns of President Richard Nixon, the mystery of number stations and how magic and filmmaking are somewhat alike.

SM: What were your backgrounds in film? How did you each grow up interested in that?

Rosentrater: I spent three years at the Colorado Film School in Denver at Colorado University. I didnÕt finish all the course work. I finished all the film classes and then ran out of interest. So, I didnÕt finish the film school program, but before I went into film school, IÕd already been working and doing a lot of editing, mostly corporate commercials in Colorado. But, IÕd always been more interested in music and mostly editing probably becauseÑa lot of it just came from the music, enjoying and seeing how it works with the music in a rhythm. I wasnÕt as much into the performances, the acting, the directing.

So, that was my background. I went to film school for three years, moved down and just watched a lot of movies and still do.

SM: What kind of editors would you watch in a row?

Rosentrater: That came later. That actually came as a result of film school. I never realized that I was interested in editing as part of the process, but during film school, I had one really great professor. I went in to do cinematography, and he said, ÒYouÕre an editor, and youÕve always edited, so why not just stick with that?Ó He made me realize that itÕs kind of the bottom. Everything ends up going there. There are a lot more interesting things that you can do on the back end of it.

He gave me a book; it was an interview of Sam OÕSteen, who was an editor who did a lot the films of Mike Nichols, Roman Polanski, all kinds of guys. So, I started reading about him. He got to the point where he was in with the directors, and they would call him to the set and say, ÒAre you going to use this shot?Ó And, heÕd say, ÒNo.Ó It would save them money and save them time. I didnÕt realize that an editor could have that much of an influence.

So, between my professor encouraging me and that, I decided, ÒThatÕs really significant.Ó So, I started watching all of his films, and also Tony Lawson, who does a lot of Neil Jordan. His first three movies were like Cross of Iron--

Kennedy: No way!

Rosentrater: Straw Dogs andÉI canÕt remember the other one. Oh, Bad Timing, which are all editing intensive. That turned me onto Nicolas Roeg and his movies with the rhythm, tone and pacing. So thatÕs where I landed when I met Carson.

[EditorÕs Note: After 1971Õs Straw Dogs and before 1977Õs Cross of Iron, Lawson also edited Stanley KubrickÕs Barry Lyndon.

KennedyÕs middle initial is ÒCÓ for Carson. Of course, IÕd forgotten to ask this at the time, and for a second imagined that Rosentrater and Kennedy had faux names for one another, much as in Gus Van SantÕs Gerry. The logical thought naturally comes last.]

Kennedy: I went to Georgia State University and graduated in 2001 with a degree in English Lit and a minor in journalism. My interest has always been writing.

My dad was a big film buff, and so we would watch a zillion old movies growing up. So, I always had the love of it. I never really thought about going into filmmaking as much just because I thought, ÒThis is so long a process to get anything done whereas if I write, I can write short stories; I can do journalism, and future pieces so itÕs there if somebody wants them done.Ó You donÕt have to go through all this other riggamarole.

I started working at the video store. I started running movie theaters. I ran a lot of different independent movie theaters around Atlanta. I ran CineFest for Georgia State while I was there for a year and a half, two years. The first year of the Midtown Arts Cinema I was the assistant manager there. Getting those places started, being a projectionist, IÕve always had that. I always tied my love of movies into what I was doing but never really thought that thatÕs what IÕd want to do creatively necessarily. But, then while I was working, I got some work on a really bad, crappy movie where I worked as a peon, or PA, for the art department, which was a great experience and I met good people but the movie is absolutely awful. While I was working on that, I got embittered about how much waste goes into one production, how for something so bad these great sets are built. ItÕs like, ÒWow, thatÕs amazing. Someone could really do something with that if they werenÕt working from an awful script.Ó

So, I was a little embittered about that, and a piece occurred to me, and the way that it occurred to me, I think that the form fits the story. YouÕve got the story, but it really plays best in a particular form. And, when I wrote (Exclusive), it just plays best as a short film. It wouldnÕt make sense to read it on the page. (laughing) And, I donÕt know if it makes sense to watch it now...So, anyway, I wrote that, and thatÕs the one I pitched to him.

After that, when I moved to Mobile, we worked long distance on it, and I really enjoyed the collaboration. And, when I was in Mobile, I was working at a bookstore, and I ran across some free photos of NixonÕs visit. At first I was just going to mess around to learn what (Marcus) does, just to get a better connect of what he goes through. So, I started taking the pictures and putting them around, and I thought I was just going to make some silly mock documentary or write a silly script and just really make it an editing experiment for myself.

Then I started researching, and I get kind of obsessive about things when I get into research and finding out about something. So, I started researching more and more, and thereÕs nothing that I could have made up that would have been stranger than the actual events themselves. TheyÕre more interesting. And, so I decided, ÒWhy not just make a sardonic documentary about it?Ó

SM: One of the biggest charges right now against young, independent filmmakers, and IÕve had this conversation with others, is that we are of an apolitical generation. And so, what was funny was that when I was watching this, I wasnÕt struck by the underlying politics. I just thought it was funny and was responding to the humor. Would you guys consider Dick-George, Tenn-Tom a political short?

Rosentrater: I donÕt think I would, not in the context that youÕre talking about. I think thatÕs something we even talked about a little bit. A lot of the documentaries that are coming out now, one of the things that I donÕt like about them, is that they feel so dated. TheyÕre so relevant that they lose relevancy within a matter of months or years. We connect on a level with each other outside of that. Those things are important to us, what theyÕre covering, those documentaries, but as far as filmmaking, weÕre interested in something different.

I think we saw it as an opportunity to pack this movie full of information about all the little off-shots that come from this little meeting. All these people were involved, and you could probably dissect any political meeting or photo shoot in this way and find the same thing.

ThereÕs a slight thing that we have with Nixon. I donÕt know what it is.

Kennedy: I donÕt know why that happensÉYeah, itÕs a coincidence. ItÕs not like any obsession that I haveÑwell, before now I guess. IÕd never read a biography of him, and I donÕt like Oliver StoneÕs film.

To go back to the political question, I was more interested in telling an interesting story. Obviously, itÕs about politics and what politics can do and how one guy trying to get votes from another guy causes one of the largest earth-moving projects in human history and kind of a financial failure that we all paid for. So, thatÕs interesting to me, those elements.

IÕm always interested in people that I donÕt like more than IÕm interested in people that I do. ItÕs more interesting to explore what youÕd typically consider a bad guy, and both of those guys are pretty abhorrent. But, I find them fascinating in the sense that youÕre always looking at it from different angles and trying to see the moments when theyÕre notÑtheyÕre not evil; theyÕre human beings.

As far as current political work, I think Marcus is rightÉIn the film world of documentary, people are just tearing stuff from headlines and going out and shooting, and I donÕt always like that. I donÕt consider it bad, but like heÕs saying, if itÕs not about the current administration, or these things, you donÕt see the documentaries. I guess you do. I just get kind of tired of the clich subject matters.

Rosentrater: It also makes it difficult, and this is one thing weÕve found, for you to get into festivals and generate interest. When people watch the movie, theyÕre usually interested, and they talk about it and like it. But, people donÕt believe that youÕre making a documentary about a photo shoot that happened 20 or 30 years ago.

Kennedy: Right. (laughing) ÒWhy?Ó

Rosentrater: Whereas if you say, ÒIÕm making a movie about my sister-in-law whoÕs got a brother in the Iraq War or Hurricane Katrina,Ó thereÕs a little bit of the heartthrob thing that weÕre missing. It makes it more difficult. People have these expectations of documentary that it needs to be relevant, or thereÕs got to be a cause.

(Gideon) brought up a good point once that stuck with me when we were working in the video store. YouÕre in the video store, and youÕve got your action section, your drama section, your comedy, your directorÕs sections and all that, but youÕve got one documentary section.

Kennedy: Right, youÕve got every different section for fictional films, every sort of classification, but one section for nonfiction films. I think thatÕs a little odd.

Rosentrater: ItÕs not that the video store is influencing the filmmakers, but I think that mentality is what exists in documentary. Either youÕre making a documentary, and itÕs got a cause or purpose, or youÕre just making a fiction and having a good time. I donÕt know how we fit into that.

Kennedy: Actually the projects that weÕre working on now, especially, and the projects for the future that weÕve got planned out, I see us having an exploration in the long run. What IÕm finding fascinating is the gray area between fiction and nonfiction films. ThereÕs an area where they coexist and comment on each other as opposed to being just one way.

SM: I was just talking to AJ SchnackÉHeÕs very interested in that space. Because, like you said, outside those documentaries being made about current events, thereÕs a whole offshoot of amazing documentarians who are doing the opposite and studying those lines between fiction and nonfiction.

But, itÕs interesting because not only are you working in documentary and are thereby relegated in the video store to one section, but youÕre also working in shorts, which means youÕre relegated nowhere in the video store. So, thatÕs an interesting space. At this level, being short filmmakers, what is that space for you to negotiate?

Short filmmakers, I feel, always seem to express to me that they get the shaft, especially with run times.

Rosentrater: Yeah, thereÕs a big gap in between that you canÕt be.

Kennedy: Right, if youÕre over thirty but less than an hour, I donÕt know what you would do with your movie.

Rosentrater: And, thereÕs no money. You can push it, and thereÕs no money to get from it.

Kennedy: As far as the making of them, obviously weÕd like to turn what weÕre doing into something that we can get rid of day jobs and actually be full-time. So, to do that, you do feature length films. But, legitimately, the feature length film that we want to do I think is viable and can only be done at that length. Again, I think the story dictates the form and also dictates how long it is. We could have stretched this one out to however long, but there wouldnÕt have been any point. It works better if itÕs tight and can work as a well-oiled machine as opposed to something loose and sprawling and kind of pointless after a while. I want to be able to get in and get out. Over time, I guess IÕve become more of an impatient viewer, so if IÕm going to sit down for this, even if itÕs only 12 minutes, thatÕs 12 minutes out of my day. I want something for that.

Rosentrater: And, one thing thatÕs nice about doing the shorts, until it is a financial thing and can support you, to me itÕs just about, ÒAre you enjoying it? Are you learning?Ó ItÕs just about growth. For me itÕs about personal growth. Even when we watched [Dick-George, Tenn-Tom at the Atlanta DocuFest screening], I thought, ÒMan, I wish people could see what weÕre doing now.Ó

If our first movie were feature length, we would never, ever make another movie because it would be so terrible.

Kennedy: (laughing) Exactly!

Rosentrater: You have to get comfortable with yourself as a filmmaker, IÕm guessing, before you make a film of feature length. ItÕs such a long amount of timeÑwell, at the pace we work anywayÑ, and six months down the road, IÕm interested in a different kind of way to show an image. If you had a film like that, it would be chaotic, if you had a film that was moving all over the place and didnÕt have any kind of consistency and style.

Kennedy: The other thing with the length as far as a writer, I enjoy short stories as much as I enjoy novels, and so I think they should be able to be self-contained. Not everything has to be an hour and a half. Not every story takes that long to tell.

SM: Now, I want to talk about some of the exact cuts. The first really funny sequence for meÑand, the cuts are used to throw the joke away, which I thought was brilliantÑwas the story about the cufflinks. [While in Mobile for the speech, Nixon lost one of his cufflinks while shaking hands. A teen found it and wrote to the President to ask permission to keep it. Punch line, of course, he asked for it back.] How did you guys stumble upon that particular piece of information, and how did you later figure out how to do the rhythm for the cut of that?

Rosentrater: Should we expose ourselves?

Kennedy: The dirty secret of it? Oh, sureÉWell, two things: Like I said, I get obsessive about research, and so I found it in one chronicle of when he was there in a Chamber of Commerce magazine that had a lot of pictures in it. So, it was there, and I didnÕt have to dig too far for it. But, I did go through and find newspaper clippings that had pictures of her.

Rosentrater: Deborah Norman.

Kennedy: I was going to try to track down her high school yearbook, but I think we had enough, he had enough to do that one sequence. The dirty secret weÕre talking about is that while I wrote it like that and intended it, Nixon did eventually send her a different pair of cufflinks and a compact as a consolation prize. Now, our point was that he still asked for the one back, and itÕs still a jerk move. I donÕt care who you are. YouÕre the President; let it go. Those should mean more to her than they are going to mean to you.

Rosentrater: It was just one, right?

Kennedy: It was a cufflink. ThatÕs kind of cool. Even though she might be able to use the presidential compact, butÑ

Rosentrater: She didnÕt have to call him.

Kennedy: Yeah, she didnÕt have to write and offer it back. She should have just kept it, and [Nixon] decided to go, ÒNah, letÕs go have those back. Send this back. Here you go.Ó Have the secretary send off some random crap that theyÕd got stored in a drawer some place for just such a letter.

Rosentrater: One thing that made that joke work was that I found that picture. I think the picture was of him flying to China, and heÕs in serious thought about something else. ItÕs like heÕs contemplating, ÒShould I ask for them back?Ó

Kennedy: I only got (Marcus) the pictures of the one where the cufflinkÕs missing, all the stuff from the actual parade and of Deborah Norman. But, there was a stand-in shot he found of a different girl writing a letter. ThatÕs not actually her, but it illustrates the point. So, he found that and the Nixon shot in the plane to tell the story of the letter writing. I think thatÕs what made it work.

SM: The other really funny section was actually listening to the conversation between Ms. Wallace and President Nixon. [After the assassination attempt on George Wallace, Nixon called with condolences. Poorly, we might add.] [You said at the screening] that it took you a long time to actually track that audio down, but what was your reaction the first time you heard that piece of audio?

Kennedy: I called Marcus right away, and I couldnÕt believe that IÕd found it. WeÕd already made the movie. We were done. I still have DVDs that are printed up with the wrong time. It was eight and a half minutes. We still have the set like that. So, I called him up when I found it, and I was freaking out. I was so glad, and as soon as he heard it, he knew. We had the exact same reaction, like ÒWell, weÕve got to open it up. We canÕt not have this in there.Ó And, heÕd filed everything away on his computer. It was a hassle; it was a can of worms, but we just had to.

Rosentrater: ItÕs really hard to understand. If we didnÕt have the text up there, it would probably be hard. (Nixon) has such a low voice on the phone, and thereÕs so much static. So the first time I heard it, I was like, ÒItÕs pretty good,Ó but then (Gideon) started pointing out a lot of the subtle jabs and how we could extenuate those a little bit by having the text up there with a little spacing.

SheÕd be like, ÒWell, heÕs going to be running out there in November.Ó SheÕs really grinding on him. And, he says, ÒWell, Teddy Roosevelt finished his speech when he was shot.Ó

Kennedy: What a jerk thing to say.

And, she even mentions the trip that had been taken. She mentions the white suit from the trip to Mobile. So, that was great. It was like, ÒOh, my God!,Ó and we tied it in there. That was just amazing, and all these little things like that. ÒHowÕs his spirit?Ó And, sheÕs like, ÒWellÉÓ ÒOh, thatÕs good to know.Ó

Rosentrater: It was like he was waiting for her to say it wasnÕt good.

SM: You can imagine him drinking his coffee and reading a magazine.

Kennedy: Exactly!

Rosentrater: And, I think he had butterscotch. When you listen to the conversation with Colson, it sounds like heÕs got a piece of candy in his mouth. HeÕs calm plotting this staged materialÑ

Kennedy: And, heÕs sucking on a mint. Every bit of him talking in that part just cracks me up in a new way every time I watch that segment. Or, thereÕs that sequence where she says something to the effect of, ÒWell, heÕs not doing too good.Ó And, (NixonÕs) like, ÒYeah?ÓÉAnd, he flubs. He just canÕt talk straight.

Rosentrater: HeÕs a Porky Pig. That one line, he says, ÒDid they have anyÑuh, did they have anyÑuh, did they do an x-ray yet?Ó

We all laugh here.

Rosentrater: What? Are you a president?

SM: The x-ray question was pretty funny considering, he says, ÒIs he conscious? Did they do an x-ray?Ó What is that leap? DoesnÕt the x-ray come a little later down the line?

Rosentrater: I think what he wanted to ask, ÒIs he going to die?Ó

Kennedy: Exactly!

SM: Thinking about human fallibility, these conversations happen every day. They donÕt necessarily happen between a president and George Wallace always but just between normal people. Are they as funny? Is the conversation only funny because you know who these people are and what their positions are?

Kennedy: Obviously, and on the side of that, I donÕt know why we got hooked on (Nixon.) He showed up in (Exclusive), and, what we liked about the first one is that although itÕs a fictional film, what I salvaged from it from a writing standpoint, is based on a lot of fact. It was about tabloids and tabloid media, the rise of tabloid media and about paparazzi, and so thereÕs a lot I liked that I found in my research that I put in there.

It was Nixon who was responsible for tabloids being in the grocery store aisles. His friend was Gene Pope Jr. who owned The New York Inquirer that became The National Inquirer. Nixon got the heads of fourteen different supermarket chains in the White House so that they could meet with Gene Pope Jr. so that he could tell them, ÒYou need to sell my magazine on your check-out aisles.Ó So, when you go to check out at the grocery store, thatÕs why those racks are there. You look at all those tabloids because of Nixon.

Rosentrater: DidnÕt he calm them down too? Up until that point theyÕd been crime scene murder photos.

Kennedy: It was some celebrity stuff, but it wasnÕt all celebrity. It was about murders with photos on the front page, anything to get someone to buy the paper. But, what was interesting is that supposedly, youÕll be hard pressed to find a National Inquirer that ever had a bad word to say about Richard Nixon.

Rosentrater: Oh, smart. I never thought about that.

SM: Throughout Watergate?

Kennedy: I havenÕt researched that. I donÕt know if thatÕs true or not. ButÑ

Rosentrater: Every media had a trademark, and The National Inquirer didnÕt have a niche on that.

To bring it back to the political relevancy, I think that there is, though it doesnÕt have the seedy music and slow motion clips of George Bush, [a sense], maybe not intentionally, that this stuff happens all the time. ThereÕs relevancy just by being there. One example of this is that I showed it to a professor of mine back at school, and what he started talking about was, ÒImagine in 20 years what someone could do with Good GoatsÑwhatÕs the book called?ÑGod Goats, the book that President Bush was reading while the 9/11 attacks were happening.

[EditorÕs Note: Referenced here is Siegfried Engelmann and Elaine C. BrunerÕs The Pet Goat.]

He was like, ÒAll that was, was a photo shoot.Ó You could really use that as a basis and have all these other things that are going on. ItÕs the same kind of thing. He enters the school, same exact plot as ours, same storyline, and the fact that he thought about it brings up a lot about that connection between people in the political environment, how much everybody knows each other.

SM: Even looking outside of a political perspective, somebody could take a picture, say right here, near the brick wall, of the three of us, and do the same thing. Even though the meaning would be different obviously, because of the situation and because these people are recognized, the interconnectivity within this group, weÕve no idea about 20 years down the road. We may all find ourselves having spoken with the same people at different points. I donÕt want to make this a sort of meta situation whereby one moment encapsulates what every other moment can be or should be, or where the potentiality of this moment is definitive of every other particular moment.

Kennedy: WeÕve made the analogy to tributaries, these branches or offshoots of time, and weÕre trying to figure out how to visualize this with what will be our fourth piece, which IÕm researching and writing. How to visualize it in a different way, to try to map or define these connections where, yeah, if youÕre at this point at this time, it extrapolates out to all these different people and this different area. The common line is a life.

Rosentrater: One thing in film school, they were always empathizing just to simplify. Simplify. I can see the value of that, but I think we have a lot of competition doing documentaries and doing shorts. People will go and watch 24, and apparently, itÕs got four different screens that youÕre watching, and theyÕre all packed with their own little subplot. People are ready to watch and take in a lot of information. So, I think the idea of simplifying a plot is kind of ridiculous at this point. I think thatÕs why in our stuff we just try and cram as much in there and keep it short. Pace comes in, but itÕs taking all those tributaries and exploring them. Some people would say, ÒWell, thatÕs not your story. Just leave it on the side, and focus on your story.Ó ThatÕs good, but what weÕre interested in is finding a way to bring in all those connections. They define that moment so much more when you understand who this person is and their relationship.

Kennedy: The one weÕre working on now does a lot of that. It crosses back-and-forth between a documentary model that goes from 1940 to the present day, exploring the history of number stations.

Number stations are spy radio stations basically. ItÕs not conspiracy theory. TheyÕre short wave radio broadcasts of people reading random strings of numbers. So, theyÕre broadcasting from home governments to spies in the field. Why theyÕve used this short wave technology up until today is that you can broadcast halfway around the world with a short wave radio. They use an unbreakable one-time pad, a single use cipher so it canÕt be broken if used properly.

Rosentrater: ItÕs the only unencryptable method of encryption.

Kennedy: Everybody in any country can walk into a store and buy a short wave radio over the counter. ItÕs really hard to triangulate where a short wave radio broadcast is being picked up as opposed to where itÕs being broadcast from. So, itÕs used by most governments, and again it sounds like real conspiratorial stuff, but itÕs not. This is used as evidence against (spies) in cases, and thatÕs where the history that we track isÑ1940 to the present, the history of spies having this as evidence used against them in their trials with each decade as theyÕve been busted.

HereÕs the popular example, Yankee Hotel Foxtrot, the Wilco album, the numbers being read on that, thatÕs a number station. They used the same recorders weÕll probably be using.

So, it follows that, and the narrative half is the one guyÕs story on his relationship with his father, whoÕs a numbers station and short wave radio enthusiast, and the gulf of communication between them. How did I put it? ItÕs put better on the site. We just wrote a new encapsulation. As it now sounds, I say that because I donÕt want to ruin it. If I tell you what itÕs really about that just ruins the movie, but if I leave it at that, it sounds like some really bad NPR-guy-talking-about-his-dad story, and itÕs not. ItÕs about how personal fantasies can turn your personal heroes into public villainsÑsomething like that.

SM: Last questions: What is one question about your filmmaking that youÕve always wanted to be asked but never have been?

Kennedy: Maybe that one. Right back at you.

No, give me a minute.

I donÕt know if IÕve really thought it out too much. I try not to think of those questions because in (Exclusive), he has a diatribe against celebrity culture and projecting your personal dreams on other people and how that can be really destructive. HeÕs basically accusing the audience of dreaming about being interviewed or dreaming about being on talk shows.
